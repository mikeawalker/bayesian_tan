Part 2: The 3-Dimensional Case¶

Obviously, aircraft motion does not occur in two dimensions. While the two dimensional case is instructive to learn about Bayesian fitting relative to a map it is not sufficient for real world adaptation.

As in part one serveral simplifying assumptions are made to the scope of this problem:

  • The aircraft is maintaining steady level flight.
  • Altimeter measurements are generated by flying a simulated trajectory over the earth and sampling terrain height from the GTOP digital elevation model which is sampled every 30 arc seconds around the Earth (30 arc seconds is equivalent to 0.008333 degrees).

3-D Equations of Motion¶

Assuming steady level flight the equations of motion for the aircraft are:

$$ \theta(t) = \theta_0 + V_\theta \cdot t \\ \phi(t) = \phi_0 + V_\phi \cdot t \\ V(t) = V_0 \\ \gamma(t) = \gamma_0 \\ V_\theta(t) = V_0 \cdot \cos( \gamma ) \cdot t \\ V_\phi(t) = V_0 \cdot \sin( \gamma ) \cdot t \\ H(t) = H_0 \\ M = f( \theta , \phi ) $$

Where the variables of interest are defines as:

Variable Description
$\theta$ Latitude
$\phi$ Longitude
$V$ The magnitude of velocity along the map
$\gamma$ Heading of the vehicle
$V_\theta$ Time derivative of $\theta$
$V_\phi$ Time derivative of $\phi$
$H$ Altitude above mean sea level
M Sampled map height based of $\phi$ and $\theta$

It is worth noting that these equations are only valid on subsections of the Earth where motion can be approximated relative to a flat Earth. By keeping the area of flight somewhat small these approximate equations hold and can be used to simplify the problem. For a real aircraft the equations would be slightly more complex.

3-Dimensional Bayesian Network¶

Based on the equations of motion a Bayesian network can be defined that will stochiastically model the equations of motion.

The problem can be started by definined uniform priors for $\phi_0$, $\theta_0$ and $\gamma_0$.

$$ \phi_0 \sim U( \phi_{L0} , \phi_{U0}) \\ \theta_0 \sim U( \theta_{L0}, \theta_{U0}) \\ V_0 \sim U( V_{L0} , V_{H0}) $$

It is possible to fit the trajectory using priors for these values as well for the values of $V_0$ and $H_0$. However, this will require significantly more samples to be taken during the markov chain monte carlo. For the sake of keeping convergence times short this projects scope will be limited by assuming known values of $H_0$ and $V_0$. Leveraging a GPU in a future project could alevate this simplifying condition. Similar to the 2-dimensional case the selection of uniform priors bounds the search space to something achievable.

An non-informative gamma distribution will also be selected as the prior for precision hyperparameter of the altimeter.

$$ \sigma_H \sim \text{Gamma} \left( 100 , 1 \right) $$

The equations of motion can be applied deterministically:

The equations of motion defined above can be applied deterministically to these parameters as follows:

$$ \theta = V_0 \cos \gamma \cdot t + \theta_0 \\ \phi = V_0 \sin \gamma \cdot t + \phi_0 \\ M = f( \theta, \phi )\\ \mu_A = H_0 - M $$

The likelihood function for the altimeter measurements is then given as:

$$ L( A | \mu_A , \tau ) \sim N\left( \mu_A, \tau \right) $$

This model is extremely close in nature to the 2-dimensional case

Numerical Conditioning¶

The choices for numerical conditioning are largely similar to the 2-dimensional case.

  • Time $t$ is non-dimensionalized such that it always runs from 0-1
  • Velocity $V_0$ is therefore a measure of how far the aircraft flies during the time of interest. This means "fast trajectories" cover more ground in this format.
  • Altitude $H_0$ is measured in kilometers (km)
  • $\theta_0$ is latitude in degrees
  • $\phi_0$ is longitude in degrees
  • $\tau$ is in units of $1/m^2$

The difference in units must be corrected for when plugging $\tau$ into the likelihood function however is helpful to keep the problem numerically conditioned.

Understanding the priors and the search space¶

To understand the search space and priors it is again important to consider the priors for $\phi_0$ and $\theta_0$ in terms of map pixels instead of in terms of engineering units (degrees or radians). In the two dimensional case the search space bounded by the uniform prior is a linear range of pixels. That can be translated directly to degrees. The map being used has a resolution of $30 \text{ arc-seconds}$ or $ 0.008 \frac{deg}{pixel}$.

2-D search space in pixels and degrees

In the three dimensional case the initial search space bounded by the prior is rectangular and that means that the number of pixels required to explored a space scales with $\mathcal O(x^2)$. This means that a search space of 1 degree latitude by 1 degree longitude contains:

$$ \left( 1 \text{deg} \cdot \frac{1 pixel }{ 0.008 deg} \right) ^2 = 15625 \text{ pixels} $$

This means that the three dimensional case requires many more samples to properly explore the search space than the one dimensional case. Therefore, for the sake of keeping computational time short in this project, these values will be assumed as known $H$, $\gamma$. It is also somewhat reasonable to assume that these values might be known or have an estimate that is known to high precision. Barometric altimeters and precision compasses are common on aircraft and UAVs, are effective at measuring these values from the local environment.

3-D search space in pixels and degrees

It is worth noting that while the search space needs to be greatly reduced 1 degree of latitude is equivalent to approximately $111 km$ that means that the search space will still be quite large in the examples explored in this problem. For example the length of the entire San Francisco Bay Area from North-South is less than $111 km$.

For whatever reason the GPS is out but the pilot uses a radar altimeter and begins measurement after it exits it's takeoff sequence. The pilot measures the heading as approximately north east and turns on the radar altimeter. Based on the following altimeter data where did the aircraft start exactly?

Summary of 3-D Estimation¶

Estimation of steady level flight trajectories work well in three dimensions. The model used is somewhat restricted in that $\gamma$ and $H$ are known. However, more variablity could be added with a larger sample size. The first case was unrealistic because of the 2-dimensional assumption. In the 3-dimensional case the assumptions are more realistic. A random flight track from United Airlines shows that once cruising altitude is achieved the steady level flight assumption is reasonable. Altitude and velocity are somewhat constant but are most certainly "piecewise constant" which means that flight could be broken into a series of steady level flight conditions.

However there are small changes in altitude and velocity. An autoregressive model should be inspected to characterize these changes.
